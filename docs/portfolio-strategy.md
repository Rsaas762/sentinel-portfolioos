# Portfolio Strategy — getting hired with Sentinel PortfolioOS

This is practical advice for turning the platform into interviews and offers. The
tool gives you structure; this doc helps you fill it well and honestly.

## 1. Lead with proof, stay honest
Recruiters skim. Your homepage hero and three featured case studies do most of the
work. Every claim should be backed by a case study, a repo, or a credential. Never
inflate — being caught exaggerating costs you more than a thin section ever would.

## 2. Write case studies that show thinking
For each project, fill all six sections:

- **Problem** — what real need or curiosity drove it. One honest paragraph.
- **Solution** — what you built, at a high level.
- **How it's built** — the stack and one or two interesting decisions.
- **Security considerations** — *this is your edge in cybersecurity.* Name the
  trade-offs. It's fine (good, even) to say what you'd improve.
- **What I learned** — specific, not generic. "I learned idempotency matters" beats
  "I learned a lot."
- **Screenshots** — add real ones as soon as you can; visuals build trust.

Tip: use the **AI assistant** to turn rough notes into a first draft, then edit it
in your own voice. It will not invent facts — but *you* must keep it honest.

## 3. Curate ruthlessly
Six strong projects beat twelve weak ones. Feature your best three on the homepage.
Mark work honestly as `live`, `in_progress`, `completed`, or `archived`.

## 4. Make security your differentiator
You're competing with generic full-stack portfolios. Emphasise:
- Threat thinking (what could go wrong, and what you did about it).
- Honest scoping ("a learning lab, isolated, not production").
- Documented controls (RLS, validation, least privilege) — which this very app
  demonstrates. You can literally talk about *its* security in an interview.

## 5. Tune skills and certifications honestly
- Proficiency is 1–5 and self-assessed. A 5 means repeated hands-on use, not one
  tutorial. Reviewers can tell.
- Show certifications **in progress** and **planned**, not just earned — it signals
  momentum and honesty.

## 6. Tighten the CV page
The CV page mirrors your content. Keep the summary to 3–4 lines, lead experience
bullets with action verbs, and make sure the printable version fits cleanly.

## 7. Drive people to contact you
- Keep the "available for work" badge accurate.
- Add your real GitHub/LinkedIn in **Settings** (and the profile).
- Check the **Messages** inbox; reply quickly.

## 8. Before you publish — checklist
- [ ] Replace ALL sample content (including the fictional "Alex Rivera" profile,
      projects, testimonials, and certifications) with your own.
- [ ] Every external link works and points to *your* repos/demos.
- [ ] No exaggerated claims; in-progress work is labelled as such.
- [ ] Screenshots added where possible.
- [ ] Light and dark themes both look right; mobile looks right.
- [ ] Connect Supabase and create your admin login.
- [ ] Custom domain + HTTPS.

## 9. Where to share it
Your CV header, LinkedIn, GitHub profile README, email signature, and job
applications. For freelance, link directly to the most relevant case study.
